Understanding Flange Facing Types:

Raised Face (RF): Most common facing with 1.6mm (1/16") or 6.4mm (1/4") raised surface. Provides better gasket compression and sealing. Used in 90% of industrial applications.

Flat Face (FF): Entire flange face is flat without raised portion. Used with full-face gaskets. Common in cast iron flanges and low-pressure glass-lined equipment.

Ring Type Joint (RTJ): Oval or octagonal groove machined in flange face. Uses metal ring gasket. Mandatory for high-pressure oil & gas applications above 900# class.

SS Grade Selection for Flanges:

SS 304/304L Flanges: General purpose austenitic grade suitable for most corrosive environments including food processing, chemical handling, and general industrial piping. 304L (low carbon) prevents carbide precipitation during welding.

SS 316/316L Flanges (Marine Grade): Contains 2-3% molybdenum for superior resistance to chlorides, acids, and marine environments. Mandatory for seawater systems, coastal installations, pharmaceutical equipment, and chemical processing. 316L preferred for welded applications.

SS 321 Flanges: Titanium-stabilized grade preventing intergranular corrosion in high-temperature service (400-800°C). Used in heat exchangers, furnace parts, and expansion joints.

SS 347 Flanges: Niobium-stabilized grade with excellent high-temperature strength. Used in pressure vessels and chemical processing equipment exposed to elevated temperatures.

SS 904L Flanges: Super austenitic grade with exceptional corrosion resistance to sulfuric acid, phosphoric acid, and severe marine environments. Used in FGD systems, desalination plants, and critical chemical processing.

Complete SS Flange Types & Selection Guide

What is a Slip-On Flange?

Design Concept: Slip-on flanges have a bore slightly larger than the pipe OD, allowing the pipe to slip into the flange. The pipe is then welded to the flange from both inside and outside (fillet welds). The raised face provides a 1/16" or 1/4" raised surface for gasket seating.

Key Characteristics:
  • Ease of Installation: Easy to align and position before welding
  • Cost: Most economical flange type (approximately 30-40% cheaper than weld neck)
  • Strength: Lower fatigue strength compared to weld neck due to discontinuous stress flow
  • Welding: Requires two fillet welds (inside and outside)
  • Pressure Rating: Suitable for Class 150, 300, 600 (not recommended for higher classes)
Best Applications for SORF:
  • Low-Pressure Systems: Water distribution, HVAC piping, compressed air
  • Non-Critical Services: General plant utilities, cooling water, low-pressure steam
  • Static Service: Applications without thermal cycling or vibration
  • Budget Projects: When cost is primary concern and service conditions permit
  • Small Bore Piping: 2" and below in low-pressure applications
Installation Guidelines:
  1. Ensure pipe OD matches flange bore tolerance (typically 1/16" clearance)
  2. Slip flange over pipe end until pipe is flush with or slightly below raised face
  3. Tack weld to hold position and check alignment
  4. Complete outside fillet weld first (pipe OD to flange hub)
  5. Complete inside fillet weld (pipe ID to flange bore)
  6. Grind welds smooth if required for flow or appearance
Limitations: SORF flanges are NOT recommended for:
  • High-pressure applications above 600# class
  • Systems with severe thermal cycling or temperature shocks
  • Services with high vibration or fatigue loading
  • Critical oil & gas applications (use weld neck instead)
  • Radiology-required piping (discontinuous stress in weld area)
When to Choose SORF:

✓ Budget is primary constraint
✓ Service conditions are non-critical
✓ Pressure class 150-600
✓ Static or minimal thermal cycling
✓ Easy field alignment is important

What is a Weld Neck Flange?

Design Excellence: Weld neck flanges feature a long tapered hub that gradually transitions from the pipe wall thickness to the flange thickness. This tapered design provides superior stress distribution and excellent fatigue resistance. The bore is machined to match the pipe schedule, and connection is made via a single full-penetration butt weld.

Engineering Advantages:
  • Superior Strength: Highest structural integrity among all flange types
  • Stress Distribution: Tapered hub design eliminates stress concentration points
  • Fatigue Resistance: Excellent performance under cyclic loading and thermal cycling
  • Leak-Proof: Full-penetration butt weld provides hermetic seal
  • RT Compatible: Weld joint can be radiographed for quality assurance
  • All Pressure Classes: Suitable from 150# to 2500# and higher
Mandatory Applications for WNRF:
  • Oil & Gas: Offshore platforms, refineries, pipelines, wellheads
  • High Pressure: Any application above 900# class
  • High Temperature: Steam systems, thermal oil, heat transfer fluids
  • Thermal Cycling: Systems with frequent temperature variations
  • Critical Process: Petrochemical plants, ammonia, hydrogen service
  • Power Plants: Boiler feedwater, main steam, high-energy piping
  • Cryogenic: LNG, liquid nitrogen, cryogenic storage
  • NACE Service: Sour gas (H2S), corrosive environments
Installation Requirements:
  1. Bevel Matching: Ensure pipe bevel and flange bevel angles match (typically 30°-37.5°)
  2. Root Gap: Maintain proper root opening (1/16" to 1/8") for penetration
  3. Alignment: Critical - use line-up clamps to ensure perfect alignment
  4. Tack Welding: Minimum 3-4 tack welds, check root gap after each tack
  5. Welding Procedure: Follow qualified WPS (Welding Procedure Specification)
  6. Multiple Passes: Root pass, fill passes, cap pass as required
  7. PWHT: Post-Weld Heat Treatment may be required for certain grades/thicknesses
  8. NDT: Radiography or ultrasonic testing as per code requirements
Cost vs. Performance:

While WNRF flanges cost 30-60% more than slip-on flanges, they provide:

  • ✓ 3x better fatigue life in cyclic service
  • ✓ Reduced long-term maintenance and downtime costs
  • ✓ Essential for code compliance in critical applications
  • ✓ Insurance against catastrophic failures
  • ✓ Only option for high-pressure/temperature service
Industry Best Practice: When in doubt, use weld neck flanges. The additional cost is minimal compared to the risk of failure in critical service. WNRF is the default choice for all oil & gas, power generation, and chemical processing applications.
Hub Dimensions Matter:

The tapered hub provides smooth stress transition. Hub length and taper angle are precisely calculated per ASME B16.5 to ensure optimal stress distribution. Never modify or machine the hub profile - it compromises structural integrity.

What is a Blind Flange?

Function: A blind flange is a solid disc with bolt holes but no bore opening. It's used to permanently or temporarily close the end of a piping system, vessel, or valve. The solid face blocks flow completely and can withstand full system pressure.

Common Applications:
  • Pressure Testing: Close system ends during hydrostatic or pneumatic testing
  • Future Expansion: Close branch connections reserved for future tie-ins
  • Maintenance Access: Provide cleanout or inspection points that can be opened and resealed
  • Vessel Closures: Close openings on pressure vessels, heat exchangers, reactors
  • Pipeline Isolation: Permanently close abandoned pipeline sections
  • Pump/Valve Testing: Close discharge during pump testing or valve maintenance
  • Safety Isolation: Block hazardous lines during plant turnarounds
Types of Blind Flanges:

Raised Face Blind (BLRF): Standard blind flange with 1/16" or 1/4" raised face. Most common type for general applications.

Flat Face Blind (BLFF): Entire face is flat. Used with full-face gaskets or when mating to flat-face equipment.

RTJ Blind: Oval or octagonal groove machined in face for ring gasket. Used in high-pressure oil & gas applications.

Spectacle Blind: Figure-8 shaped device with one solid plate (blind) and one open ring (spacer). Can be rotated to open or close flow. Used for positive isolation.

Spade & Spacer: Separate solid disc (spade) and ring (spacer). Similar function to spectacle blind but as individual pieces.

Thickness Considerations:

Blind flanges must be thick enough to resist full system pressure without deflection. Thickness increases with:

  • Larger diameter (pressure force = pressure × area)
  • Higher pressure class
  • Lower material strength

Always use blind flanges rated for the system pressure class. Never use a lower-class blind flange with higher-class mating flange.

Installation Best Practices:
  • Use appropriate gasket type matching the facing (RF, FF, RTJ)
  • Ensure bolt length accounts for blind flange thickness
  • Apply lubricant to bolts and nuts
  • Tighten bolts in star pattern to ensure even gasket compression
  • Use torque wrench for critical applications
  • Never exceed rated pressure for the blind flange
Safety Note: Before removing a blind flange, always verify system is depressurized and drained. Blind flanges under pressure can become deadly projectiles if bolts fail. Use proper lockout/tagout procedures.
Advantages of Blind Flanges:
  • ✓ Provides positive shutoff - no leakage
  • ✓ Can be installed and removed without cutting pipe
  • ✓ Suitable for all pressure classes
  • ✓ Economical compared to valves for permanent closure
  • ✓ Available in all materials and sizes

Socket Weld Flanges (SWRF)

Design: Similar to slip-on but with a counterbore (socket) that accepts the pipe. Pipe is inserted into socket leaving small gap at bottom, then fillet welded on outside only.

Advantages:

  • Smoother bore transition than slip-on (better flow characteristics)
  • Easier alignment than butt weld
  • Only one weld required (outside fillet)
  • Less skill required than butt welding

Applications:

  • Small bore piping (2" and under typically)
  • High-pressure small bore (up to 2500# class)
  • Instrumentation and sample lines
  • Process analyzer piping

Critical Installation Detail: Must leave 1/16" (1.5mm) expansion gap at bottom of socket before welding. This prevents cracking due to thermal expansion during welding and service.

Lap Joint Flanges (LJRF)

Design: Flat ring flange used in conjunction with a stub end. Stub end is welded to pipe, lap joint flange slides over stub end. Flange can rotate freely for easy bolt hole alignment.

Advantages:

  • Easy bolt hole alignment (flange rotates independently)
  • Economical when expensive alloys required (only stub end in alloy, flange in carbon steel)
  • Facilitates frequent dismantling for inspection/cleaning
  • Reduces cost in systems requiring exotic materials

Applications:

  • Systems requiring frequent dismantling (food, pharmaceutical)
  • Clad or lined piping where expensive alloy only needed at pipe
  • Misalignment-prone installations
  • Rotating equipment connections where alignment is difficult

Limitation: Not suitable for high-pressure/temperature applications. Maximum recommended Class 300.

Threaded Flanges (THRF/Screwed)

Design: Female NPT or BSP threads machined in flange bore. Pipe with male threads is screwed into flange. No welding required.

Advantages:

  • No welding required (useful where hot work not permitted)
  • Can be installed/removed without cutting pipe
  • Useful in explosive atmospheres where welding is hazardous
  • Good for temporary piping or test setups

Applications:

  • Small bore low-pressure piping (typically 3" and under)
  • Non-critical utility services
  • Temporary or test piping
  • Areas where welding is prohibited
  • Quick coupling/decoupling requirements

Limitations:

  • ✗ Not suitable for cyclic or fatigue loading (threads can loosen)
  • ✗ Risk of leakage at threads in vibrating service
  • ✗ Not recommended for flammable/toxic services
  • ✗ Limited to lower pressure classes (typically 150-300)
  • ✗ Threads can corrode and seize in corrosive environments

Installation: Apply thread sealant (PTFE tape or pipe dope). Tighten using pipe wrench on pipe, not flange. Do not over-tighten which can crack female threads.

Orifice Flanges

Purpose: Special flanges designed to hold orifice plates for flow measurement. Feature jack screws and special machined facing.

Features:

  • Jack screw holes to spread flanges for orifice plate insertion/removal
  • Machined facings to precise flatness for orifice plate seating
  • Alignment pins to ensure orifice plate orientation
  • Pressure taps for differential pressure measurement

Applications: Flow measurement in oil & gas pipelines, chemical process lines, custody transfer metering.

Reducing Flanges

Purpose: One flange with two different nominal bore sizes. Eliminates need for separate reducer fitting and flange.

Example: 6" x 4" reducing flange has 6" bolt pattern and facing but 4" bore for pipe connection.

Advantage: Saves one welding joint, reduces installation time and cost, fewer potential leak points.
